More information

■Monocytosis is an increase in the number of monocytes circulating . Monocytes are white blood cells that give rise to macrophages and dendritic cells in the immune system.

■Infectious mononucleosis (IM, mono), also known as glandular fever, is an infection usually caused by the Epstein–Barr virus (EBV).

（A picture about mono）

MHC class I

CD8 cytotoxic T cells kill infected cells by recognizing foreign pathogen derived peptides that are transported to the cell surface bound to MHC class I molecules. The peptides carried by MHC class I molecules come from the degradation of proteins in the cytosol, and so cytotoxic T cells act against pathogens whose proteins are found in the cytosol of the host cell at some stage in their life cycle.

Cytotoxic T cells kill their infected targets with great precision and neatness, by inducing apoptosis in the infected cell while sparing adjacent normal cells; this strategy minimizes tissue damage .

CD8 cytotoxic T cells release two types of preformed cytotoxins which seem able to induce apoptosis in any type of target cell, and the protein perforin, which is thought to act as a translocator, pore-forming protein to enable granzymes to cross the membrane of the target cell.

EBV (also known as human herpesvirus 4) is a member of the virus family Herpesviridae. It has a double-stranded linear DNA genome enclosed in an icosahedral capsid and a lipid envelope, it replicates its DNA genome in the host -cell nucleus. EBV infects only humans and is one of the most successful infectious agents on its obligate host. The virus infects mainly B cells and epithelial cells.

（EBV under magnification）

Analysis of single EBV-infected cells from tonsils of IM patients for rearranged immunoglobulin genes revealed two strategies of EBV for rapid and massive spread in the B cell compartment:

The direct infection of many naive as well as memory cell.

Germinal center B cells and the expansion of the latter cells to large clones.

Infected B cells are stimulated to secrete immunoglobulin, producing, among other antibodies whose detection is one of the most widely used diagnostic tests for EBV infection. This heterophile antibody is not specific for EBV antigens but binds to antigens present on heterologous red blood cells and agglutinates them.

Case study

(a bad sore throat from a B-cell infection)

Emma Bovary

15-year-old female with severe sore throat , fever and malaise.

Her throat was so swollen and she had difficulty swallowing.

Her anterior and posterior cervical lymph nodes were swollen and tender (lymphadenopathy).

Her abdomen felt soft and the liver was enlarged.

Emma’s 1-year-old brother

became ill at the same time, but did not have such severe symptoms. He was merely listless and felt warm. He had no particular physical symptoms, and seemed to recover completely after a few days.

Overall

clinical signs and symptoms

At least 3 of the following symptoms are positive:

fever

pharyngitis, tonsillitis

cervical lymph node enlargement (> 1cm)

the liver enlarged

Splenomegaly

Treatment

She received 1 liter of normal saline intravenously followed by 20 mg of methylprednisolone (a corticosteroid) intravenously every 12 hours. Her throat culture again proved negative for streptococcus but her blood serum was positive for lgM and lgG antibodies against EBV capsid antigen.

Emma improved quickly with the symptomatic treatment and was discharged on the second day after admission to complete her recovery at home.

Question

Why the symptoms are different?

Answer

The signs and symptoms of infectious mononucleosis vary with age.

Children

Before puberty, the disease typically only produces flu-like symptoms, if any at all. When found, symptoms tend to be similar to those of common throat infections

Adolescents and young adults

In adolescence and young adulthood, the disease presents with a characteristic triad:

Fever

Sore throat

Swollen glands
